\large Network Dependence, Observation Grain, and Recoverability\\

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Pandemic transmission depends on contact network structure. The mean-field ODE SIR
model is therefore strictly misspecified as a mechanistic account: the $SI/N$ term
encodes uniform mixing, not actual contact topology. But misspecification alone does
not determine whether the network mechanism is empirically recoverable. The central
question is whether the observation process---the grain at which infections are
measured and the clock at which they are reported---contains enough information to
identify network structure.

We demonstrate that under weekly aggregate incidence, it does not. Data are generated
from a network SIR process with known community structure; a mean-field ODE is
calibrated to those observations. The ODE posterior concentrates near a pseudo-true
parameter that absorbs network structure into a lower effective aggregate rate,
understating the counterfactual reference by 23\% in our baseline experiment.
Collecting more independent outbreaks at the same grain and clock reduces posterior
width fourfold without reducing bias. More data make the wrong model more certain
when the additional data are collected under the same insufficient observation process.
We connect this to PAC-Bayesian regularization: the ABM must allow heterogeneity,
but the data---given their grain and clock---determine whether that heterogeneity
is supported or should remain uncertain.
\end{abstract}

\section{The Problem}

Agent-based modelers routinely simulate epidemic spread, opinion dynamics, and
market behavior at the level of individual agents \citep{epstein2008}. The
simulation produces rich internal trajectories: who interacted with whom, in what
order, under what conditions. But the data available for calibration are almost
always coarser. A modeler fitting an epidemic model to weekly case reports compares
a fine-grained stochastic process to aggregate counts measured at a weekly clock.

This mismatch creates a problem that is easy to overlook. When the model is
calibrated to coarse data, estimated parameters may be fitting the observation
process as much as the underlying mechanism. If the observation process discards
information that the mechanism depends on, fitted parameters will not recover the
true microscopic rates---no matter how much data are collected.

Because network dependence is assumed to be mechanistically relevant, the central
inferential problem is not whether the mean-field model is exactly true. It is not.
The problem is whether the observation process is rich enough to distinguish
network-dependent transmission from a lower-dimensional mean-field approximation.

\section{Contribution}

We study when network structure in an agent-based epidemic model is empirically
recoverable from observed epidemic data. We assume that pandemic transmission is
network-dependent, so the mean-field SIR model is strictly misspecified as a
mechanistic account. However, misspecification alone does not determine whether
the network mechanism can be learned. Recoverability depends on the observation
process: the grain at which infections are measured and the clock at which they
are reported.

We use simulated network SIR epidemics to vary observational grain and clock. Under
rich observations, network structure can be recovered using regularized inference.
Under coarse aggregate observations, the same latent process supports only a reduced
mean-field approximation, and additional data at the same grain make that
approximation more certain without recovering the missing network mechanism. We
connect this to PAC-Bayesian regularization: the ABM must allow heterogeneity, but
the data determine whether that heterogeneity is supported or should remain uncertain.

We formalize this by treating agent-based models as generative statistical models
with two components:
\[
\text{simulator} \;\to\; \text{latent social process} \;\to\; \text{observation process} \;\to\; \text{data.}
\]
The simulator encodes a theory of how agents interact. The observation process
encodes how those interactions are measured---at what resolution, with what
reporting rate, and with what aggregation structure. A simulation is not yet a
statistical model until both components are specified.

\subsection*{Observational Grain and Clock}

We distinguish observational grain from observational clock. Grain refers to the
level at which the latent process is measured: individual infection events,
group-level incidence, network-edge exposure histories, or aggregate population
counts. Clock refers to the temporal resolution at which those measurements are
reported: daily, weekly, event-time, or irregular reporting intervals. In the
baseline experiment, the observation process maps a continuous-time network epidemic
into weekly aggregate incidence counts. This discards both network position and
within-week timing.

\section{The SIR Example and the Social Assumption}

The standard mean-field ODE SIR model \citep{kermack1927, keeling2008} is:
\[
\frac{dS}{dt} = -\beta_{ODE}\frac{SI}{N}, \qquad
\frac{dI}{dt} = \beta_{ODE}\frac{SI}{N} - \gamma I, \qquad
\frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I.
\]
The term $SI/N$ is often treated as algebra. It is not. It encodes a strong social
assumption: susceptible-infected contact opportunities are proportional to the
product of aggregate counts. This is uniform mixing. \emph{The product term is a
social assumption.} Mean-field SIR is not wrong because it is mathematically crude;
it is wrong because it encodes a social theory of uniform contact.

\section{Network SIR and SI Edges}

A network SIR model replaces uniform mixing with an explicit contact structure
\citep{keeling2008, kiss2017}. For susceptible agent $i$ with neighbors
$\mathcal{N}(i)$ in contact network $A$, the infection probability over a short
interval follows from the competing-hazards structure of independent Poisson
contact processes \citep[Ch.~3]{kiss2017}:
\[
P\!\bigl(i \text{ infected in } [t,\,t+\Delta t]\bigr)
= 1 - \exp\!\Bigl(-\beta\,\Delta t \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \mathbf{1}\{X_j(t)=I\}\Bigr).
\]
Here $\beta$ is the per-contact, per-infected-neighbor transmission rate---not the
same parameter as $\beta_{ODE}$. Aggregate incidence depends on $E_{SI}(t)$, the
number of susceptible-infected edges \citep{newman2002}, not directly on the
product $SI$.

A critical point about scale: $\beta$ and $\beta_{ODE}$ are not directly
comparable. Under \emph{uniform} mixing with mean degree $\bar{d}$, each
susceptible has on average $\bar{d} \cdot I/N$ infected contacts, so the
aggregate infection rate is $\beta \cdot \bar{d} \cdot SI/N$. An ODE fitted to
data from such a network should therefore recover:
\[
\beta_{mf,true} = \beta \cdot \bar{d}.
\]
$\beta_{mf,true}$ is not a microscopic ground truth. It is a calibration benchmark:
the ODE transmission rate recovered when the same per-contact process is run on a
uniform network with the same mean degree and observed through the same reporting
scheme. We validate this benchmark empirically: fitting the ODE to data from an
actual uniform network with mean degree $\bar{d} \approx 12$ recovers $\beta_{ODE}$
within 2\% of $\beta_{mf,true}$.

Under community structure, the approximation $E_{SI} \approx c\,SI$ fails. The
epidemic burns rapidly through the seed group, then stalls at group boundaries
before spreading slowly across sparse between-group edges. This two-phase dynamics
produces an aggregate incidence curve that no single-$\beta_{ODE}$ ODE can match
exactly. The ODE instead concentrates its posterior around a pseudo-true
$\beta_{ODE}^*$ that best approximates the overall pacing of the aggregate curve.

The bias arises from fitting a structurally misspecified mean-field model to data
whose observation process has discarded the network information needed to distinguish
community transmission from a lower effective aggregate rate. The sources of failure
are distinct: (1)~structural misspecification (the ODE assumes uniform mixing);
(2)~grain loss (aggregate counts discard group and network position); (3)~clock
loss (weekly reporting discards within-week timing); and (4)~parameter translation
($\beta$ in the network model is not $\beta_{ODE}$). The gap $\beta_{ODE}^* -
\beta_{mf,true}$ reflects community structure operating through all four channels.

\section{Regularization and the Uniform Graph}

We generate contact networks from a stochastic block model \citep{holland1983}:
\[
\operatorname{logit} P(A_{ij}=1) = \alpha + \eta\, B_{ij},
\]
where $B_{ij} = 1$ if agents $i,j$ share a group and $B_{ij} = -1/(K-1)$
otherwise. The parameter $\eta$ controls community structure; $\alpha$ controls
overall density. The regularization penalty is $\lambda\eta^2$.

At $\eta = 0$, all dyads have equal edge probability $\sigma(\alpha)$: the graph
is Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi and mixing is uniform. The uniform graph is not the neutral
case. It is the maximally regularized case: the network that remains when all
group-based distinctions have been shrunk to zero. The standard ODE is
correspondingly the maximally regularized aggregate limit. It is not what happens
when an ABM gets large. It is what happens when the ABM gets large, coarse, and
socially uniform.

The role of regularization is not to eliminate heterogeneity from the ABM.
Heterogeneity is part of the model's theoretical content: agents differ in contact
neighborhoods, and epidemic transmission depends on the resulting susceptible-infected
edges. Rather, regularization determines which forms of heterogeneity are supported
by the data. In the stochastic block model, $\eta$ parameterizes group-based
network structure, while the penalty $\lambda\eta^2$ shrinks unsupported group
structure toward the uniform graph. Thus the uniform graph is not treated as
substantively true; it is the limiting case obtained when the data do not justify
retaining group-level contact heterogeneity.

\section{Simulation Results}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We simulate $N=1000$ agents in $K=4$ equal groups. The community network uses
$\alpha=-4.4$, $\eta=2.5$ (mean degree $\approx 37$, within-group edge share
$\approx 0.89$). True parameters: $\beta = 0.04$~day$^{-1}$ (per contact),
$\gamma = 0.10$~day$^{-1}$ (mean infectious period 10 days). The mean-field
reference rate is $\beta_{mf,true} = \beta\bar{d} \approx 1.47$. We simulate
150 days, observe weekly aggregate incidence (21 time points), and fit the ODE
via grid posterior with $\gamma$ fixed. Let $Y_k$ denote the observed new-infection
count in week $k$ and $\hat{Y}_k$ the corresponding ODE-predicted count; the
likelihood is:
\[
\log(1 + Y_k) \sim \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\log(1+\hat{Y}_k),\,\sigma^2\bigr).
\]

\paragraph{Bias result.}
The ODE posterior concentrates near $\beta_{ODE}^* \approx 1.13$, well below the
counterfactual reference $\beta_{mf,true} \approx 1.47$---the value the ODE
\emph{would} recover from a uniform network of the same density. The bias is
$-0.33$, or 23\% of the reference. The community epidemic's two-phase shape
(rapid within-group spread, slow between-group spread) cannot be reproduced by
any single-$\beta_{ODE}$ ODE; the posterior instead concentrates on the
$\beta_{ODE}$ that minimizes aggregate misfit.

Standard posterior concentration and acceptable aggregate fit can coexist with
mechanistic failure. The fitted ODE may track weekly incidence while its parameter
absorbs unobserved network structure into a biased effective rate.

\paragraph{More data, same grain.}
We generate $R$ independent community-network outbreaks and pool their likelihoods.
As $R$ increases from 1 to 20, the posterior 95\% credible interval width shrinks
from 0.136 to 0.036---a fourfold reduction. The posterior MAP stays in the range
$[1.11,\,1.22]$, always well below $\beta_{mf,true} \approx 1.47$.

\paragraph{Regularization path.}
Varying $\eta$ from 0 to 3 and pooling 20 independent outbreaks per level to
obtain stable pseudo-true estimates, ODE bias becomes increasingly negative as
community structure strengthens: $-0.044$ (9\% of reference) at $\eta=0$ and
$-0.48$ (23\% of reference) at $\eta=3$. Absolute bias increases convexly: modest
for small $\eta$ and accelerating as community structure becomes strong. As
community structure strengthens, the epidemic increasingly spreads in group-wise
waves; the ODE compensates by fitting a lower effective transmission rate.

\section{PAC-Bayesian Inference and Recoverability}

In a PAC-Bayesian formulation \citep{mcallester1999}, candidate network structures
or network-generating parameters are evaluated by predictive performance penalized
by departure from a prior over simpler or more regular mechanisms. Under rich
observation---such as individual infection times or sufficiently fine group-level
incidence---the posterior should concentrate near the correct network structure or
community parameter. Under coarse observation---such as weekly aggregate
incidence---the posterior should remain diffuse over network structures or
concentrate only on reduced effective parameters.

PAC-Bayes lets the model recover structure when the data support it and quantify
uncertainty when they do not. This is the appropriate inferential logic for
agent-based models: retain heterogeneity when the data support it, penalize it
when they do not, and quantify uncertainty when the observation process cannot
decide. A mechanism cannot be validated at a resolution where its identifying
information has been discarded. More data help only when they add information
relevant to the mechanism, not merely more replications of the same reduced
observation.

This framing clarifies the role of priors. A prior is a form of regularization:
it encodes which model configurations are penalized in the absence of data support,
exactly as a penalty term shrinks parameters toward a simpler structure
\citep{tibshirani1996}. Priors and perturbation schemes are in this sense
dual---both express assumptions about which departures from a baseline model should
require justification from the data \citep{hazan2016}.
The practical consequence is that disputes over which prior is \emph{correct} are
largely unproductive. No prior is uniquely correct; each encodes a different
regularization. What is productive is comparing priors: if conclusions are stable
across a range of reasonable priors, they are robust to that modeling choice. If
they are not, the instability is itself informative about what the data can and
cannot identify \citep{gelman2013}.

A practical workflow follows: (1)~specify the latent process and observation
process separately; (2)~compare richer simulations against their mean-field limits;
(3)~penalize unnecessary heterogeneity in agents and network structure; (4)~vary
observation grain and clock; (5)~examine posterior bias; (6)~run posterior
predictive checks.

\section{Conclusion}

Mean-field SIR is not wrong because it is mathematically crude; it is wrong because
it encodes a social theory of uniform contact. Whether that wrongness matters
empirically depends on the observation process. Fine-grained data can support
network recovery. Coarse data may only support a reduced aggregate model.
PAC-Bayesian regularization provides a way to distinguish these cases: retain
heterogeneity when the data support it, penalize it when they do not, and quantify
uncertainty when the observation process cannot decide.

Under weekly aggregate incidence, additional outbreaks reduce posterior variance
around a pseudo-true ODE parameter but do not recover the network mechanism. More
data at the same grain and clock estimate the wrong reduced model more precisely.
The fully qualified claim is this: more data make the wrong model more certain when
the additional data are collected under the same insufficient observation process.
Richer grain or finer clock data can recover network structure; coarse data require
regularized uncertainty rather than false mechanistic confidence.
